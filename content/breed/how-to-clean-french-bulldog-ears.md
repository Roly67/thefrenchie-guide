Those ears. Those glorious, enormous, satellite-dish bat ears that make French Bulldogs look like they're permanently trying to pick up radio signals from distant galaxies.

## Why French Bulldog Ears Need Extra Attention

Not all dog ears are created equal. Frenchies have open, upright ears that act like funnels — catching dirt, dust, pollen, and whatever else is floating about. Compare that to a Spaniel's floppy ears, which at least have the decency to keep some debris out (though they trap moisture instead).

**French Bulldog ears are vulnerable because:**

- **Wide open shape** — catches airborne particles, grass seeds, and dust
- **Narrow ear canals** — despite the big outer ear, the canal inside is relatively narrow, making blockages more likely
- **Allergy-prone breed** — Frenchies are notorious for allergies, and ear infections are often the first sign
- **Warmth** — the ear canal is warm and dark, making it a five-star hotel for yeast and bacteria
- **Wrinkles at the base** — where the ear meets the head, moisture can collect in skin folds

Jago's ears need more attention than Buster's. Whether that's genetics, his tendency to shove his head into every hedge he encounters, or just bad luck — he produces more ear wax and gets itchier if we skip a week.

## Step-by-Step: How to Clean French Bulldog Ears

### Step 1: Get Comfortable (Both of You)

Choose a time when your Frenchie is relaxed — after a walk or during a calm evening is ideal. We do ears on the sofa during evening telly time. The boys are half-asleep and compliant.

Have your cleaner, cotton pads, and treats within arm's reach.

### Step 2: Inspect First

Before you start cleaning, have a look inside each ear. You're checking for:

**Normal (carry on with cleaning):**
- Light pink skin
- Minimal wax (slight yellowish tinge is fine)
- No smell
- No redness or swelling

**Concerning (clean gently, monitor):**
- Moderate brown wax buildup
- Slight redness
- Mild odour

**Vet visit needed (don't clean, book appointment):**
- Dark brown or black discharge
- Strong, unpleasant smell (yeasty, cheesy, or foul)
- Significant redness or swelling
- Dog is in obvious pain when you touch the ear
- Head tilting or persistent scratching
- Bleeding or crusty scabs

If something looks seriously wrong, don't try to fix it yourself. The vet needs to see what's going on before you potentially push infection deeper.

### Step 3: Apply the Cleaner

Hold the ear flap gently upward. Squeeze a few drops of ear cleaner into the ear canal — you don't need to flood it, just enough to coat the surface.

**Buster's reaction:** Stoic acceptance. A single sigh. Continues sleeping.

**Jago's reaction:** Tries to shake immediately. Hence the towel.

### Step 4: Massage the Base

With the cleaner in, gently massage the base of the ear (where it meets the head) for 15-20 seconds. You should hear a squelching sound — that's normal and means the cleaner is working its way through the canal.

This is the bit most dogs actually enjoy. Buster practically purrs. Jago tolerates it with the air of someone enduring a necessary indignity.

### Step 5: Let Them Shake

Now step back (or protect yourself with the towel) and let them shake. They will shake. Vigorously. This is physics doing the heavy lifting — the shaking brings loosened debris up and out of the ear canal.

The first time Buster shook after ear cleaning, he sprayed ear cleaner across the living room wall. The cat was not impressed. We've since learned to do this on a wipe-clean surface.

### Step 6: Wipe Clean

Using a cotton pad, gently wipe the visible inner ear, the ridges, and the area around the opening. Don't push anything into the canal — just clean what you can see.

Fold the pad around your finger for better control in the ear's folds and ridges.

**Replace the pad when it gets dirty.** You'll typically need 2-3 pads per ear.

### Step 7: Don't Forget the Base Wrinkle

Where the ear meets the head, there's often a small fold or wrinkle. This traps moisture and gets gunky. Wipe it clean and dry it thoroughly. This is a common spot for irritation that people miss.

### Step 8: Treat and Praise

Treats. Praise. More treats. You want ear cleaning to be associated with good things, not a wrestling match.

Both boys now see the ear cleaner bottle and position themselves for the routine. Pavlov would be proud (again).

## How Often Should You Clean French Bulldog Ears?

**Weekly** is the sweet spot for most Frenchies.

- **Weekly:** Standard maintenance for healthy ears
- **Twice weekly:** If your Frenchie swims, has allergies, or produces excess wax
- **After every swim or bath:** Water in the ear canal = infection risk
- **Less than weekly?** Only if your vet specifically says so

Over-cleaning can strip natural protective oils and cause irritation. Under-cleaning leads to buildup, odour, and infection. Weekly hits the balance.

We clean ears every Sunday evening. It's part of the grooming routine: wrinkles, ears, paw check, done. Takes about 10 minutes for both dogs.

## Recognising Ear Infections

Even with regular cleaning, Frenchies can develop ear infections. Knowing the signs early saves money, stress, and suffering.

### Yeast Infections (Most Common)

- **Smell:** Sweet, musty, bread-like (think warm bakery, but wrong)
- **Discharge:** Brown, waxy
- **Other signs:** Head shaking, scratching, rubbing ears on carpet
- **Cause:** Moisture, allergies, compromised immune system

### Bacterial Infections

- **Smell:** Strong, unpleasant, almost rotten
- **Discharge:** Yellow or green, sometimes bloody
- **Other signs:** Pain when ear is touched, swelling, redness
- **Cause:** Often secondary to another issue (allergies, foreign body, ear mites)

### Ear Mites

- **Discharge:** Dark brown/black, crumbly (looks like coffee grounds)
- **Other signs:** Intense scratching, head shaking
- **Cause:** Parasites — can transfer between pets
- **Note:** Less common in adult dogs, more common in puppies

### What to Do

Don't attempt to treat infections yourself with over-the-counter products. Get a vet diagnosis. They'll determine the type of infection and prescribe the right treatment — antibiotic drops for bacterial, antifungal for yeast, anti-parasitic for mites.

Buster had one ear infection in his life. It was yeast-based, likely triggered by a food allergy we hadn't identified yet. A week of antifungal drops and a food switch sorted it. Since then — touch wood — clean ears all round.

## Ear Cleaning and Allergies: The Connection

If your Frenchie has recurring ear problems despite regular cleaning, allergies are likely the culprit. Ears are often the first place allergy symptoms show up in French Bulldogs.

**Common allergy triggers:**
- Food allergies (chicken, beef, dairy, grains — see our [dog food guide](/best-dog-food-french-bulldogs-uk/))
- Environmental allergies (pollen, dust mites, mould)
- Contact allergies (certain shampoos, cleaning products, grass types)

Jago's ears flare up in spring — pollen season. We clean twice weekly from March to June and it keeps things under control. If it gets worse, it's a vet visit for antihistamines.

**Signs it's allergy-related:**
- Recurring infections despite clean routine
- Seasonal pattern to ear problems
- Other allergy symptoms present (itchy skin, paw licking, sneezing)
- Ear problems started after a food change

If you suspect food allergies, an elimination diet under vet guidance is the gold standard. We switched Jago's food and his ears improved noticeably within three weeks. Coincidence? Possibly. But we're not switching back.

## Tips From Three Years of Ear Cleaning

### Make It Routine

Same time, same place, same order. Dogs thrive on predictability. Our Sunday evening grooming session is now so routine that both boys assume the position without being asked.

### Warm the Cleaner Slightly

Cold liquid in the ear is unpleasant. Hold the bottle in your hands for a minute before use, or run it under warm (not hot) water. This small thing makes a big difference to their tolerance.

### Check After Walks in Long Grass

Grass seeds can lodge in Frenchie ears and cause serious problems — pain, infection, even abscesses. After woodland or field walks, do a quick visual check. If you see a seed, try to gently remove it with tweezers. If it's deep, vet visit.

### One Ear at a Time

Clean one ear fully before starting the second. It's easier to track what you've done, and the dog gets a brief break between ears.

### Partner Up If Needed

If your Frenchie is a wriggler, one person holds and distracts while the other cleans. We managed solo from early on because we started young, but there's no shame in needing a second pair of hands.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if my French Bulldog's ears are dirty?

Look inside. If you see brown wax buildup, smell something musty, or your dog is scratching more than usual — it's time for a clean. A little pale yellow wax is normal and healthy.

### Can I use baby wipes to clean my Frenchie's ears?

Not ideal. Baby wipes can leave moisture in the ear canal and may contain fragrances that irritate. Use a proper ear cleaning solution or vet-approved ear wipes designed for dogs.

### My Frenchie hates ear cleaning. What can I do?

Go slower. Start by just touching the ears and rewarding. Then holding the ear flap and rewarding. Build up gradually over days or weeks. High-value treats (cheese, chicken) make the bribery more effective. If your dog becomes genuinely aggressive or distressed, consult a behaviourist.

### How do I clean a French Bulldog puppy's ears?

Same process, but gentler and with smaller amounts of cleaner. Start ear handling from the first week at home so they grow up tolerating it. See our [puppy checklist](/french-bulldog-puppy-checklist/) for the full first-week routine.

### Should I pluck ear hair?

French Bulldogs don't typically have significant ear hair (unlike Poodles or Schnauzers). If you notice hair in the canal, ask your vet or groomer — they'll advise whether plucking is needed. Don't attempt it yourself without guidance.

### Can dirty ears cause head tilting?

Yes. Ear infections can cause head tilting, balance issues, and disorientation. If your Frenchie is tilting their head persistently, it's a vet visit — not a cleaning issue.
